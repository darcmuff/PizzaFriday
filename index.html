How to Use

This is a pizza dough calculator. Set your sliders, choose your style, and it works out everything — ingredient quantities, yeast levels, fermentation times, and a step-by-step method with real clock times. Every slider has a green zone showing the range where results are most predictable. The ends fade to red — not because they are wrong, but because they need more experience to manage well.

Start With the End

When do you want to eat? Work backwards from there. The Total Time slider is how many hours from mixing to eating. Set it, and the calculator figures out everything else — the yeast quantity, your fermentation times, and the method steps. Longer fermentation develops deeper flavour and better browning. A cold ferment of 24–72 hours produces the best results — the enzymes in the flour stockpile free sugars and amino acids while the dormant yeast is not consuming them, and that biochemical stockpile is what drives the Maillard reaction in the oven. But a same-day 8-hour dough still makes great pizza.

Neapolitan vs New York

Neapolitan — lower hydration (58–65%), higher salt (2.5–3%), no oil. Baked fast at extreme heat (300°C+). The result is a soft, pliable base with a puffy, charred cornicione. Traditionally uses Tipo 00 flour, though any strong bread flour with 12–14% protein works. This is a simple dough — flour, water, salt, yeast, nothing else.

New York style — moderate hydration (58–65%), standard salt (2–2.5%), with olive oil (1–3%). Baked at lower temperatures (230–260°C) for longer. The oil coats the gluten strands, shortening the network and creating a crispier, more tender crust. Use high-gluten bread flour with 13–15% protein for the best structure and chew. NY dough needs to be strong enough to hold toppings and fold without cracking.

Dough Balls

Set the count and size using the steppers. 250g balls give you a 25–28cm (10–11") pizza — a good default for Neapolitan. 280g balls stretch to 30–35cm (12–14") for NY style. The calculator uses baker's percentages to work backwards from your ball weight to the exact flour, water, salt, and yeast quantities.

What Flour?

Flour strength matters. The W-value measures how much punishment a flour can take — how much water it can absorb and how long it can ferment without collapsing. A weak flour (W150–200) is fine for a same-day bake but will fall apart during a 48-hour cold ferment. For long fermentation, use a strong flour (W260–320) or very strong Manitoba-type (W320+) with 13–15% protein. If you do not know your flour's W-value, check the protein content on the label — higher protein generally means stronger flour.

Hydration

Hydration is the ratio of water to flour. For pizza, the range is much narrower than for focaccia. 58–65% is the sweet spot for both styles. Lower hydration (55–60%) gives a stiffer dough that is easier to handle and produces a crispier base. Higher hydration (63–70%) gives a softer, more extensible dough with a lighter crumb, but it is harder to shape and more likely to stick.

If you are new to pizza making, start at 60–62%. Work up as your handling improves. Do not confuse pizza hydration with bread hydration — a 65% pizza dough is a completely different animal to a 65% focaccia.

Salt

Salt is not optional. It strengthens the gluten network by allowing the protein strands to cross-link more tightly. Without it, the dough is slack, tears easily, and ferments out of control. Traditional Neapolitan uses 2.5–3% — higher than most bread recipes. The salt also creates osmotic stress on the yeast cells, which slows fermentation and gives you more control over timing. The calculator adjusts the yeast automatically when you change the salt percentage.

Oil

Neapolitan dough traditionally contains no oil. NY style uses 1–3%. Oil does not hydrate the flour — it coats the gluten strands and physically interrupts their formation, which is why it makes the crust more tender and crispy. Add oil after the flour has absorbed the water, not before. If oil hits dry flour first, it creates a hydrophobic barrier that prevents proper hydration.

Room Temp or Cold Ferment?

Room temp — all fermentation at ambient temperature. Simpler, faster, and good results. The time slider will not go past 24 hours in room temp mode — beyond that, the dough will overproof and collapse.

Cold ferment — initial rise at room temp, then into the fridge. At fridge temperature (4°C), yeast drops to about 15% activity, but the flour's amylase and protease enzymes only slow by about 50%. So while the yeast sleeps, the enzymes keep breaking down starches into sugars and proteins into amino acids. This is the secret to superior browning, blistering, and complex flavour. The time slider will not go below 14 hours in cold mode.

Temperature

Temperature is the master variable. A 2°C change can alter yeast activity by up to 25%. The calculator compensates automatically — just set the slider to your kitchen temperature. The yeast model uses a Q10 biological coefficient: yeast roughly doubles its metabolic rate for every 10°C increase, up to its thermal death point around 55°C. If your kitchen is warm, you need less yeast. If it is cold, you need more. The maths handles this for you.

Baking

Hit ▶ Begin to stamp your start time and turn the calculator into a live schedule. Every step shows its actual wall-clock time. Hit ☰ Timeline for the full overview.

Everything recalculates live — change a slider mid-bake and the remaining steps update automatically. If you close the app, the session is saved. Hit ↺ Reset to start fresh.

Timers

Steps that need timing show a ⏰ timer pill. Tap it and your phone's native clock app opens with the correct duration. Important: native timers only work in the Android app. On the website, the pills show as progress indicators but will not launch external timers.

The Oven

As hot as it goes. Real Neapolitan pizza is baked at 450°C+ for 60–90 seconds. Your home oven will not reach that, but get it as hot as possible — 250–300°C with a pizza stone or steel preheated for at least 45 minutes. The stone stores thermal energy and transfers it directly into the base, which is what gives you a crisp bottom and proper oven spring.

A pizza steel is better than a stone — steel conducts heat roughly 20 times faster. If you are serious about pizza, it is the single best investment you can make.
